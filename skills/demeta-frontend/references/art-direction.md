# Art direction and originality

Use this reference to turn a real subject into a coherent visual world. Originality is not random novelty; it is a specific relationship between content, material, interaction, and conversion.

## Brief precedence

Resolve conflicts in this order:

1. Explicit user requirements and owned brand assets.
2. Existing product design system, accessibility behavior, and repository conventions.
3. Factual content, audience, locale, and conversion constraints.
4. Inferred art direction.

Do not reject a dark brand because a heuristic prefers light. Do not change recurring brand tokens merely to look novel. Compare novelty in the concept layer: hero architecture, spatial organization, primary object, material metaphor, interaction, and narrative sequence.

## Niche intelligence

Write concrete lists before visual concepts:

- **Artifacts:** objects, tools, rooms, documents, equipment, ingredients, surfaces, products, screens, routes, specimens, or measurements.
- **Rituals:** what practitioners and customers repeatedly do, in sequence.
- **Proof:** what reduces doubt—process visibility, provenance, outcomes supplied by the user, certifications supplied by the user, live states, or craft details.
- **Anxieties:** what can go wrong and what reassurance the interface must provide.
- **Sensory cues:** weight, friction, transparency, pressure, temperature, sound, softness, speed, silence, light, grain, scale.
- **Conversion moment:** the exact action and what the visitor expects next.

Reject nouns that could fit any industry. A "dashboard," "network," or "workflow" only counts when its actual objects and states are named.

## Concept candidates

Produce three or four candidates. Use the roles `commercially-safe`, `visually-ambitious`, and `niche-native` to force different reasoning, not a fixed set of media.

Each candidate states:

- direction name that describes an idea, not only the brand;
- one-sentence thesis;
- content architecture and first-viewport composition;
- primary object or evidence;
- signature interaction;
- medium and why it helps;
- mobile/reduced-motion mutation;
- risk and containment plan;
- complete visual fingerprint.

Reject candidates that are color/font variants. Do not force characters, generated images, videos, 3D, dashboards, or scroll hijacking into every concept.

## Material metaphors

Physical behavior can become interface grammar when it belongs to the subject:

- **Flow:** water, air, ink, money, traffic, sound, heat, and data can use currents, advection, refraction, ripples, trails, or pressure.
- **Optics:** transparent products, medical imaging, trust, layers, and inspection can use glass, dispersion, cutaways, lenses, and depth.
- **Soft matter:** fashion, interiors, packaging, performance, and craft can use cloth, paper, folding, stretching, tension, and drape.
- **Assembly:** restoration, manufacturing, tools, and systems can use exploded views, section cuts, constraints, and staged reconstruction.
- **Growth:** ecology, learning, communities, and research can use branching, fields, accretion, cells, and time-based formation.
- **Spatial evidence:** places, expeditions, property, logistics, and venues can use maps, routes, depth, scale, and viewpoint.

The metaphor must carry real information or action. A refractive glass phone is useful when internal layers, repairability, or product transparency are the story; it is decoration when the offer is unrelated bookkeeping.

## Design thesis

Write:

- subject, audience, and single job;
- one remembered signature;
- one justified aesthetic risk;
- one restraint decision;
- 4–7 named color tokens with roles;
- display, body, and utility/data type roles;
- geometry, material, imagery, icon, and motion rules;
- header, hero frame, section rhythm, and mobile mutation.

Spend boldness in one system. Remove decoration that does not encode hierarchy, meaning, state, evidence, or interaction.

## Visual fingerprint

Record these exact fields for every candidate and accepted build:

1. `heroArchetype`
2. `composition`
3. `palette`
4. `geometry`
5. `primaryVisual`
6. `motionSignature`
7. `typography`
8. `sectionRhythm`
9. `headerArchitecture`
10. `heroFrame`
11. `scrollDepthSystem`

Candidate rules:

- Do not reuse both `heroArchetype` and `composition`.
- Do not reuse both `headerArchitecture` and `heroFrame`.
- Reject pairs sharing more than 45% of fingerprint values.
- Preserve explicit brand invariants; do not count them as novelty failures.

Cross-project rules:

- Compare only when a prior artifact or corpus is available.
- Treat similarity as evidence, not automatic plagiarism.
- Fail an unrelated-output clone only when at least two signals agree: categorical fingerprint, normalized DOM/layout, structure screenshot, source similarity, or visual embedding.
- Never claim global uniqueness from a local comparison.

## Anti-template detectors

Redesign architecture when any of these dominates:

- a 50/50 copy-left/media-right hero;
- centered pill navigation plus a detached corner CTA;
- headline consuming most of the viewport while evidence is small or absent;
- wallpaper media with low-opacity overlays and floating cards;
- six or more equal rounded rectangles carrying the page's visual identity;
- default purple/blue gradient, dark SaaS glass, cream/serif/terracotta, or acid-on-black cluster without subject justification;
- card grids and generic fade-up after the hero;
- decorative numbers, pills, status labels, grids, or particles;
- device or 3D object with no relationship to comprehension or conversion;
- identical desktop/mobile architecture merely stacked into one column;
- copied reference type, palette, imagery, and composition at once.

Changing colors does not fix a template. Change the information architecture or primary interaction.

## Aesthetic review

Review rendered desktop and mobile states without reading the implementation first:

1. Can the niche be recognized before reading the logo?
2. Is one signature element memorable after ten seconds?
3. Does the signature reveal evidence, process, product, or place?
4. Do type, material, spacing, geometry, imagery, and motion feel authored by one system?
5. Does the first viewport show offer, action, and meaningful evidence without clipping?
6. Does the lower page develop the idea instead of reverting to cards?
7. Does mobile feel recomposed rather than compressed?
8. Does the static/reduced-motion state still look intentional?
9. What can be removed without losing meaning?
10. Could the exact architecture survive an unrelated industry-name swap? If yes, redesign.
